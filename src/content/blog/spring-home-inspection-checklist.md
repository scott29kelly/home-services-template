Spring is the best window for a thorough home exterior inspection. Winter's freeze-thaw cycles, ice dams, wind events, and heavy snow loads stress every system on your home's envelope. Catching damage in spring — before summer heat and another storm season — gives you time to schedule repairs on your terms rather than in emergency mode after the next storm. Here is the complete checklist.

## Why Spring Timing Matters

A spring inspection hits the sweet spot:
- **Winter damage is fresh and visible** — staining, displaced materials, and cracked caulk are easier to identify before weathering obscures them
- **Repair season is starting** — contractors are booking up; early scheduling gets better availability and sometimes better pricing
- **You have time before storm season** — most regions see peak storm activity in late spring and summer; completing repairs in early spring provides a buffer

## Roof Inspection

Start with the roof — it is your home's most critical water barrier and often where winter damage concentrates.

**From the ground or attic (do not walk the roof without proper safety equipment):**

- [ ] **Shingle condition:** Look for curling, cupping, cracking, or missing shingles. Use binoculars if needed.
- [ ] **Granule loss:** Check gutters for accumulated granules — a thick layer after winter indicates accelerating shingle wear.
- [ ] **Ridge line:** The ridge should appear straight and level. Sagging suggests decking or structural issues.
- [ ] **Flashings:** Check around chimneys, skylights, vents, and valleys. Look for lifted, cracked, or missing flashing.
- [ ] **Chimney cap and crown:** Cracks in the mortar crown allow water to infiltrate and cause major damage.
- [ ] **Attic inspection:** From inside, look for daylight, moisture staining on rafters, frost residue on insulation, or soft/spongy decking.

**Red flags requiring professional attention:**
- Sagging sections anywhere on the roof plane
- Multiple missing or severely damaged shingles
- Water staining on attic rafters or decking
- Daylight visible through the roof deck

## Gutter and Downspout Inspection

Gutters take heavy abuse from ice dams, falling branches, and debris. Spring is the time to assess and clear them.

- [ ] **Clear all debris:** Remove leaves, pine needles, and other debris that accumulated since fall cleaning.
- [ ] **Check for proper slope:** Gutters should slope 1/4 inch per 10 feet toward downspouts. Standing water indicates slope issues.
- [ ] **Look for cracks, holes, or rust:** Inspect the full gutter run from ground level. Holes allow water to drip against the foundation.
- [ ] **Check gutter seams and joints:** Lap joints and mitered corners are failure points. Reapply gutter sealant where needed.
- [ ] **Inspect hangers and spikes:** Gutters pulling away from the fascia need new hangers. Loose gutters collect debris and eventually fail.
- [ ] **Test downspout flow:** Run water with a hose and watch that each downspout flows freely. Use a plumber's snake to clear clogs.
- [ ] **Downspout extensions:** Verify extensions discharge water at least 6 feet from the foundation.

## Siding and Exterior Wall Inspection

Walk the full perimeter of your home, examining each wall systematically.

- [ ] **Panel condition:** Look for cracked, split, or missing panels. Check for warping, buckling, or bowing.
- [ ] **Seam and joint integrity:** Check all horizontal laps, vertical joints, and corners. Look for gaps wider than 1/8 inch.
- [ ] **Caulk around windows, doors, and penetrations:** Crumbly, cracked, or missing caulk is an immediate repair item. Good caulk is flexible and adheres fully.
- [ ] **Paint or coating condition:** For painted siding, check for bubbling, peeling, or significant fading. Bubbling indicates moisture behind the surface.
- [ ] **Foundation clearance:** Siding should not contact soil or mulch. Maintain 6-inch clearance above grade to prevent rot and pest infiltration.
- [ ] **Window and door frames:** Look for gaps between frames and surrounding siding. Check that all flashing above windows is intact.

## Foundation and Drainage

Winter can shift soil and crack foundation elements. Spring is when drainage problems become apparent.

- [ ] **Walk the foundation perimeter:** Look for new cracks in the foundation wall or mortar joints (horizontal cracks are more serious than vertical).
- [ ] **Grade and drainage:** The ground should slope away from your foundation at 6 inches per 10 feet. Low spots against the foundation allow water pooling.
- [ ] **Window well drainage:** Make sure window wells have clean gravel beds and that wells are not filling with debris.
- [ ] **Basement or crawlspace check:** Look for moisture, puddles, or new efflorescence (white mineral deposits) on foundation walls.
- [ ] **Sump pump test:** Pour a bucket of water into the sump pit and verify the pump activates and discharges properly.

## Deck, Porch, and Exterior Wood

- [ ] **Check for rot:** Probe wood members with a screwdriver at joints, post bases, and decking boards. Soft spots indicate rot.
- [ ] **Check fasteners:** Look for raised nail heads or rust staining around screws — both indicate fastener failure from moisture cycling.
- [ ] **Railing integrity:** Test railings by applying lateral force. Building codes require railings to withstand 200 lbs of lateral load.
- [ ] **Seal or stain:** Most wood decks need resealing every 1-3 years. Spring is the ideal time before summer use begins.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I do this inspection myself, or do I need a professional?**

Most homeowners can complete the ground-level and attic portions of this inspection. The exception is roof walking — without proper footwear, safety harnesses, and experience, walking a roof creates serious fall risk. We recommend professional inspection for any roof concerns you identify from the ground or attic. For the rest of the checklist, a DIY inspection is a valuable supplement to (not replacement for) a periodic professional inspection.

**Q: How long does a professional exterior inspection take?**

A thorough professional inspection of an average home takes 1.5-3 hours. This includes the roof, exterior walls, gutters, foundation perimeter, and an attic inspection. You receive a written report with photos of any deficiencies found.

**Q: What is the most important thing to check first?**

The roof and attic, every time. Water infiltration from the roof is the most common cause of major structural damage in homes. Finding a small flashing problem in spring costs a few hundred dollars to repair. Finding it after water has been seeping into your wall cavity all summer can cost tens of thousands.
