Knowing when to repair versus replace your roof can save you thousands of dollars. Many homeowners wait too long — patching leak after leak — when a full replacement would have been more cost-effective years earlier. Here are the seven most reliable signs that your roof has reached the end of its service life.

## 1. Your Roof Is Over 20-25 Years Old

Age is the most reliable predictor of replacement need. Most architectural asphalt shingle roofs carry manufacturer warranties of 25-30 years, but actual service life depends on:

- Installation quality
- Attic ventilation (poor ventilation shortens shingle life significantly)
- Local climate and UV exposure
- Storm history

If your roof is approaching or past the 20-year mark, even if it looks okay from the street, it is likely in the final phase of its service life. Have it professionally inspected before the next storm season.

## 2. Curling or Cupping Shingles

Shingles curl in two ways:
- **Curling (clawing):** Edges lie flat but the middle lifts up
- **Cupping:** Edges turn upward while the center dips

Both patterns indicate the shingles have absorbed moisture repeatedly and are no longer bonded properly to the decking. Curled shingles are structurally compromised — they are vulnerable to wind uplift and no longer shed water effectively.

If you see curling across more than a small section, you are looking at a systemic problem, not an isolated repair.

## 3. Significant Granule Loss

Those small, sandy particles embedded in your shingles are not decorative — they protect the asphalt from UV degradation. As shingles age, granules loosen and wash away.

Signs of significant granule loss:
- Shingles look faded or unevenly colored in sections
- Granules accumulating in gutters or at downspout outlets
- Bare, shiny patches visible on shingle surfaces

A few granules in the gutters after a storm is normal. A thick layer of granules in every gutter after every rain is a warning sign. Once the granule coating is gone, UV accelerates shingle degradation rapidly.

## 4. Cracked, Broken, or Missing Shingles

Individual shingles can crack from thermal cycling (repeated heating and cooling), hail impact, or simple age-related brittleness. Missing shingles from wind events leave your roof deck directly exposed to rain.

The question is scale:
- **A few isolated shingles** can often be replaced without full replacement
- **Multiple areas of cracking or missing shingles** across different sections indicates the entire field has become brittle and unreliable
- **Cracking near ridges or valleys** is particularly serious — these are high-stress zones where water concentrates

## 5. Light Visible in the Attic

On a bright day, go into your attic and look up. If you can see daylight through the roof boards or around penetrations, water is also getting in — or will during the next heavy rain.

While you are in the attic, look for:
- Water stains on rafters or decking
- Dark streaks indicating moisture channels
- Soft or spongy decking (press the underside — it should feel solid)
- Frost on insulation in winter (sign of chronic moisture infiltration)

Daylight in your attic is never a minor issue.

## 6. Sagging or Uneven Roof Deck

A healthy roof deck should look flat and uniform from the street. Sagging or "waviness" indicates:
- Rotted or saturated sheathing boards
- Structural damage to rafters or trusses
- Long-term moisture intrusion that has compromised the decking

This is one of the most serious signs and typically means replacement is urgent — not just of the shingles, but potentially of the underlying decking as well.

## 7. Persistent or Recurring Leaks

If you have had the same area repaired multiple times and water keeps finding its way in, the problem is not isolated. Recurring leaks suggest the waterproofing system as a whole has broken down — multiple failure points exist across the roof, and patching individual spots is fighting a losing battle.

At a certain point, the cost of repeated repairs exceeds the cost of replacement, and every repair delay risks additional damage to insulation, framing, drywall, and personal property.

## Repair vs. Replace: A Practical Framework

| Situation | Recommendation |
|-----------|----------------|
| Isolated damage on a roof under 15 years old | Repair |
| Roof over 20 years with widespread issues | Replace |
| Multiple failing areas across the roof | Replace |
| Single storm damage on recently installed roof | Repair + insurance claim |
| More than 25% of shingles compromised | Replace |

When in doubt, get a professional inspection. A reputable contractor will give you an honest recommendation — and the best contractors will also tell you when a repair is all you need.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I install new shingles over my old ones?**

In some cases, yes — building codes typically allow one layer of shingles over an existing layer. However, this is rarely the best long-term approach. Installing over existing shingles hides problems, adds weight, shortens the life of the new shingles, and can void manufacturer warranties. A full tear-off gives you a clean start and lets us inspect the decking for damage.

**Q: How much does a full roof replacement cost?**

For a typical 2,000 sq ft home, asphalt shingle replacement generally runs $8,000-$18,000 depending on roof complexity, pitch, local labor rates, and materials chosen. Get 2-3 quotes from licensed contractors to understand your local market.

**Q: How long does a new roof last?**

Architectural asphalt shingles typically last 25-30 years with good installation and proper ventilation. Metal roofing can last 40-70 years. The quality of installation often matters as much as the material choice.
