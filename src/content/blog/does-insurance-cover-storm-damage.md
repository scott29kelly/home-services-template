Most standard homeowners insurance policies do cover storm damage to your roof and siding. Wind, hail, and lightning damage are typically covered under the "open perils" or "named perils" section of your policy — but the exact coverage depends on your specific plan, deductible, and whether your home was properly maintained before the storm hit.

## What Storm Damage Is Typically Covered

Standard homeowners insurance (HO-3 policies, the most common type) covers sudden and accidental damage caused by:

- **Wind and hail** — The most common claims after severe storms
- **Lightning strikes** — Including fire damage resulting from a strike
- **Falling trees or debris** — If a tree falls on your roof during a storm
- **Ice dams and freeze damage** — In states where this is a named peril

When a storm tears off shingles, dents your siding with hail, or sends a tree limb through your roof, that is the kind of sudden, accidental loss that insurance is designed to cover.

## What May NOT Be Covered

Not every damage claim is straightforward. Insurers can deny or reduce claims for several reasons:

- **Pre-existing deterioration** — If your roof was already 25 years old and showing wear before the storm, the insurer may argue the damage was from age, not the storm.
- **Neglect or lack of maintenance** — Rotted decking, rusted gutters, or improperly sealed flashing can lead to partial denials.
- **Flood damage** — Standard homeowners policies do NOT cover flooding. You need separate flood insurance through the NFIP.
- **Earthquake damage** — Also excluded from standard policies.
- **Cosmetic damage only** — Some insurers have added "cosmetic damage exclusions" for minor hail dents to metal siding or gutters that don't affect function.

## How to Check Your Policy Coverage

Before storm season, take 20 minutes to review your policy:

1. **Find the Declarations Page** — This is your one-page summary showing coverage limits, deductibles, and policy type.
2. **Look for the "Perils Insured Against" section** — This lists what is and isn't covered.
3. **Check your deductible** — Many policies have a separate, higher deductible specifically for wind or hail damage (sometimes 1-5% of your home's insured value rather than a flat dollar amount).
4. **Review ACV vs. RCV** — Actual Cash Value (ACV) pays you the depreciated value of your roof; Replacement Cost Value (RCV) pays what it actually costs to replace it with new materials. RCV policies pay out significantly more.
5. **Call your agent** — If anything is unclear, ask directly: "Does my policy cover wind and hail damage to the roof and siding?"

## Steps to Take After Storm Damage

If you believe your home has storm damage:

1. **Stay safe** — Do not climb on the roof while it is wet or immediately after a storm.
2. **Document everything** — Take photos and video of all visible damage before touching anything.
3. **Call your insurer** — Most insurers require you to report claims promptly. Delays can complicate the claim.
4. **Get a professional inspection** — Have a licensed roofing or siding contractor inspect the damage and provide a written estimate. This documentation strengthens your claim.
5. **Make temporary repairs** — If needed to prevent further damage, cover openings with tarps. Keep all receipts; temporary repair costs are often reimbursable.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Will my insurance premiums go up if I file a storm damage claim?**

Filing a single weather-related claim may or may not affect your rates — it depends on your insurer and your claims history. Weather claims are typically treated differently than liability claims. Ask your agent about your insurer's specific policy before filing.

**Q: Do I need to use the insurance company's preferred contractor?**

No. You have the right to choose your own licensed contractor. However, if you use an out-of-network contractor, confirm they will work directly with your insurance adjuster or provide the documentation the insurer needs.

**Q: How long do I have to file a storm damage claim?**

Most policies require you to report damage "promptly" or within a specific window — often 1 year from the date of loss. However, the sooner you file, the better. Delayed claims are harder to prove and may raise questions about whether new damage occurred after the storm.
