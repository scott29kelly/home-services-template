After a severe storm, act quickly but safely. The first 24-48 hours are critical for protecting your home from further water intrusion, building a strong insurance claim, and getting the repair process started. Here is exactly what to do, in order.

## Step 1: Prioritize Safety First

Before you inspect anything, make sure the area is safe:

- **Stay away from downed power lines.** Call your utility company immediately if you see any.
- **Check for gas leaks.** If you smell gas, leave the house and call your gas company from outside.
- **Avoid standing water.** It can be electrified or contaminated.
- **Do not climb on the roof** while it is wet, slippery, or immediately after the storm.

If there is any structural damage — collapsed sections, cracked foundation, broken load-bearing walls — do not re-enter until a professional has cleared it.

## Step 2: Document Everything Before Touching Anything

Good documentation is the foundation of a strong insurance claim. Before any cleanup or temporary repairs:

- **Take photos and video** of all visible damage from multiple angles — exterior, interior, any possessions affected.
- **Include wide shots** to show context (e.g., the whole roof face, not just close-ups of missing shingles).
- **Date-stamp everything.** Most smartphones automatically embed the date and time in photo metadata.
- **Write notes** describing what happened and when the storm struck.

This evidence is far harder to gather after cleanup begins. Spend 30-60 minutes documenting before you move anything.

## Step 3: Make Temporary Repairs to Prevent Further Damage

Your policy typically requires you to take "reasonable measures" to prevent additional damage after a storm. This does not mean making permanent repairs — it means stopping the bleeding:

- Cover exposed roof sections with heavy-duty tarps secured at the edges
- Board up broken windows or doors
- Move valuable possessions away from active leaks
- Use towels or buckets to manage interior water

**Keep all receipts** for materials and labor. Temporary repair costs are usually reimbursable under your policy.

Do NOT make permanent repairs or start replacing materials before your insurance adjuster has seen the damage. You could inadvertently complicate or reduce your claim.

## Step 4: Contact Your Insurance Company

Report the damage to your insurer as soon as possible — within 24-48 hours if you can. Delaying the claim can create complications.

When you call:
- Have your policy number ready
- Describe what happened and when
- Ask when an adjuster can inspect the property
- Ask whether you qualify for emergency funds for hotel or living expenses if the home is uninhabitable
- Get a claim number and document all conversations (date, time, name of representative)

## Step 5: Schedule a Professional Inspection

Before your insurance adjuster arrives, or at the same time, get a licensed contractor to inspect the damage. A contractor can:

- Identify all storm-related damage, including hidden damage the adjuster might miss
- Provide a written, itemized estimate
- Document damage with professional-grade photos
- Explain what repairs are necessary vs. cosmetic

Ask your contractor to be present when the adjuster visits if possible. Two sets of eyes catching the same damage is better than one.

## Step 6: Work With Your Adjuster

When the insurance adjuster arrives:
- Walk them through all the damage you documented
- Share your contractor's written estimate and photos
- Point out areas that may not be obvious (interior water staining, damaged insulation, hidden soffit damage)
- Take your own notes during the inspection

If you feel the adjuster's assessment is too low or incomplete, you have the right to dispute it or hire a public adjuster to advocate on your behalf.

## What to Expect Next

Once the claim is filed:
- Your insurer will issue an Explanation of Benefits (EOB) detailing what they will and will not cover
- Your deductible will be subtracted from the payout
- If you have an RCV (Replacement Cost Value) policy, you may receive an initial ACV check and a second check after repairs are complete

Work with a contractor you trust to manage the repair scope and ensure the work matches what the insurance company has approved.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I start cleaning up debris before the adjuster comes?**

Yes — cleaning up debris is generally fine and does not affect your claim. Just document everything thoroughly with photos and video first, and do not begin permanent repairs or remove damaged materials before the adjuster's visit.

**Q: What if I can't reach my insurance company right away?**

Many insurers have 24/7 claims lines. If you cannot reach your agent directly, look up your insurer's main claims phone number. In a major storm event, call volume can be high — try the online portal or app as an alternative.

**Q: Should I hire a public adjuster?**

If your damage is significant (over $20,000) or if your insurer's initial settlement seems much lower than contractor estimates, a public adjuster who works exclusively for you (not the insurance company) can sometimes recover substantially more. They typically charge 10-15% of the settlement. For smaller claims, it may not be worth the cost.
