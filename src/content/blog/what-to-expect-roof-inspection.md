Most homeowners have never been present for a professional roof inspection and are not sure what to expect. Understanding the process helps you ask better questions, evaluate the findings more critically, and make more confident repair decisions. Here is a complete walkthrough of what a thorough professional roof inspection covers.

## When to Schedule a Roof Inspection

You do not need to wait until there is an obvious problem. Schedule an inspection when:

- Your roof is 10+ years old and has never been professionally inspected
- After any significant storm event (hail, high winds, fallen trees nearby)
- When preparing to sell or buy a home
- When filing an insurance claim for storm damage
- If you notice interior signs of moisture — staining on ceilings, damp smell from attic
- Before the roof warranty expires on a newer installation

Annual inspections are not necessary for most homes, but every 3-5 years for a healthy roof in good condition is reasonable. After storm season, more frequently.

## What the Inspector Covers

A thorough professional inspection has three components: the exterior roof surface, the attic interior, and the gutters and drainage system.

### Exterior Roof Inspection

The inspector will walk the roof (weather and pitch permitting) to conduct a close examination of:

**Shingles or roofing surface:**
- Overall condition and estimated remaining life
- Curling, cupping, cracking, or blistering
- Granule loss patterns and severity
- Missing or displaced shingles
- Any areas of compression or damage from foot traffic

**Flashings (the most common leak source):**
- Chimney flashing — step flashing at the sides, base flashing, and counter-flashing
- Valley flashing — the channels where two roof planes meet
- Skylight flashing — perimeter seals and curb condition
- Pipe boot flashings around plumbing vents
- Drip edge at eaves and rakes

**Penetrations:**
- Plumbing vent boots (rubber boots that seal around pipes — they crack over time)
- Exhaust vents for HVAC, bath fans, and range hoods
- Satellite dish or antenna mounts

**Structural:**
- Ridge line appearance (should be straight)
- Roof plane flatness (sagging or waviness indicates decking or structural issues)
- Fascia and soffit condition at the eaves

### Attic Inspection

The attic inspection is equally important — sometimes more so. It reveals what the exterior inspection cannot show:

- **Moisture staining on rafters or decking:** Historical water infiltration leaves dark staining even after the source is repaired
- **Active daylight:** Visible light through the roof deck indicates open penetrations
- **Ventilation assessment:** Check that ridge vents, soffit vents, and any power vents are functional and balanced
- **Insulation condition:** Moisture-damaged insulation loses R-value and can indicate chronic infiltration
- **Decking integrity:** The inspector can press test the underside of decking to check for soft or spongy areas

### Gutter and Drainage

- Gutter condition, pitch, and securement
- Downspout connections and discharge points
- Granule accumulation (indicates shingle wear rate)
- Ice dam evidence (staining at eaves or in gutters)

## What the Inspector Delivers

A thorough inspector provides a written report, typically within 24-48 hours, that includes:

- **Condition summary:** Overall assessment and estimated remaining service life
- **Identified deficiencies:** Itemized list of issues found, with severity ratings
- **Photo documentation:** Photos of each deficiency, clearly labeled and located on a roof diagram or photo
- **Repair recommendations:** Specific work recommended to address each deficiency
- **Cost estimates (if requested):** Many inspectors will provide repair estimates as part of the same visit

Ask directly if you expect a written report — some inspectors do verbal reports only, which gives you nothing to reference later or to use for insurance purposes.

## Questions to Ask During the Inspection

The inspection is your opportunity to get expert answers to your specific concerns. Good questions to ask:

- "How many more years do you expect this roof to last with no major repairs?"
- "If you were buying this house, would any of what you found change your offer?"
- "Which of these items is urgent, which is monitor-and-address-soon, and which is long-term?"
- "Is this damage consistent with a recent storm, or does it look like pre-existing wear?"
- "What maintenance can I do annually to extend the life of this roof?"

## What a Roof Inspection Costs

- **Standalone inspection:** $150-$350 for an average residential roof
- **As part of a repair estimate:** Often complimentary — contractors inspect to quote
- **Insurance-claim inspection:** Often covered or reimbursed as part of the claims process
- **Home inspection (when buying a home):** $400-$700 covers the full home including roof

The investment is worthwhile. A $200 inspection that reveals a $400 flashing repair prevents a $4,000 water damage claim a year later.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I be present during the inspection?**

Yes, and we encourage it. Being present lets you ask questions about specific areas you are concerned about, hear the findings explained in real time, and see what the inspector sees (particularly in the attic). If something is on the roof and you cannot safely join the inspector, ask them to photograph it specifically for you.

**Q: What is the difference between a roofing inspection and a home inspection?**

A general home inspection covers all major systems — foundation, structure, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, and roof. Inspectors are generalists. A roofing contractor inspection is focused specifically on the roof system and is typically more detailed for that component. For a targeted roof assessment, especially after storm damage or before a major decision, a roofing contractor inspection is the better choice.

**Q: Do I need a roof inspection before every insurance claim?**

No, but it helps. Insurance adjusters conduct their own assessment during a claim. However, having an independent contractor inspection before or during the claims process ensures you have a second set of expert eyes identifying all damage. Adjusters work quickly and may miss items that a contractor walking the same roof more slowly would catch.
