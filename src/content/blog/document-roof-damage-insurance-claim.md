Proper documentation is the most important step in filing a successful roof damage insurance claim. Clear photos, written records, and a professional inspection report can mean the difference between a full payout and a denied or underpaid claim. Here is a systematic approach to building an airtight documentation file.

## Why Documentation Matters

Insurance adjusters are trained to evaluate claims skeptically. Their job is to assess damage accurately — which means they will look for evidence that damage was caused by the covered storm event, not by pre-existing wear, neglect, or age. Strong documentation is your best protection against a lowball settlement or outright denial.

A well-documented claim answers three key questions:
1. What is damaged?
2. How was it damaged?
3. When did the damage occur?

## Tools You Will Need

Before you start, gather:
- Smartphone with camera (adequate for most situations)
- Ladder (use with a spotter; never on a wet roof)
- Measuring tape
- Notepad or notes app
- Date of the storm (check weather service records if needed)

## Step 1: Photograph Before Cleanup

**Do not clean up anything until you have finished documenting.** Every piece of evidence matters.

Start from the street and take wide establishing shots of:
- Each face of the roof
- The entire exterior of the home
- Any obvious structural damage

Then move closer for detail shots of:
- Missing, cracked, or curling shingles
- Dented or pierced metal (vents, flashing, gutters, drip edge)
- Granule accumulation in gutters (a sign of hail impact)
- Damaged or missing ridge cap shingles
- Bent or dislodged satellite dishes or ventilation caps

**Capture video too.** A slow, narrated walkthrough is harder to dispute than still images alone.

## Step 2: Document Interior Damage

Go inside and look for signs that the roof damage has allowed water penetration:
- Water stains on ceilings or walls
- Wet insulation in the attic
- Daylight visible through the attic (a clear sign of a breach)
- Mold or mildew starting to form

Photograph all of this. Interior damage supports the claim that the exterior damage is real and actively causing harm to the structure.

## Step 3: Collect Supporting Evidence

Strengthen your documentation with:

- **Storm reports** — NOAA, your local National Weather Service office, or sites like StormAware track storm events by location and date. Print or screenshot the relevant report.
- **Neighborhood context** — Photos of nearby trees down, neighbor roof damage, or damaged vehicles can corroborate that the storm was severe.
- **Pre-storm photos** — Check Google Street View or old photos of your home to demonstrate what the roof looked like before the storm.

## Step 4: Get a Written Contractor Inspection Report

A professional inspection from a licensed roofing contractor is one of the most valuable pieces of documentation you can have. A quality inspection report includes:

- Itemized list of all damaged components with their locations
- Photos taken from the roof surface (angles you cannot safely achieve yourself)
- Estimated replacement or repair scope
- Contractor's signed statement of findings
- Materials and labor cost estimate

This report serves as independent corroboration of your claim and gives your adjuster a detailed scope to work from. If the adjuster's assessment falls significantly short of the contractor's estimate, the written report is your evidence in a dispute.

## Step 5: Organize and Submit

Create a single organized package for your insurer:

1. A written summary (1-2 pages): what happened, when, what damage you found
2. All photographs, labeled by location and date
3. Storm documentation (weather reports, news articles if available)
4. Contractor inspection report and estimate
5. Any receipts for emergency tarping or temporary repairs

Send this as a complete package when you file or when requested by the adjuster. Keep copies of everything you submit.

## Common Documentation Mistakes to Avoid

- **Starting cleanup before documenting** — You may accidentally remove evidence.
- **Only taking close-up shots** — Adjusters need context; provide wide and close angles.
- **Forgetting interior evidence** — Attic damage and ceiling stains are powerful proof of roof failure.
- **Not noting the storm date** — Claims have time limits; the date of loss must be established.
- **Making permanent repairs first** — This can complicate or void parts of your claim.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does my insurance company need the original photos, or can I send copies?**

You can send digital copies or upload directly to your insurer's claims portal. Keep the originals (on your phone or backed up to cloud storage) for your own records and in case of disputes.

**Q: What if I cannot safely photograph the roof myself?**

Do not put yourself at risk. Ask your roofing contractor to document the damage during their inspection — most will provide a full photo report as part of the inspection. This is actually more credible evidence than homeowner-taken photos.

**Q: How long should I keep my documentation after the claim is settled?**

Keep all claim documentation for at least 3-5 years. If a related issue (like hidden water damage that was missed) surfaces later, your documentation establishes the original cause of loss.
