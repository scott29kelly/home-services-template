Storm preparedness is not about paranoia — it is about reducing the cost and stress of the inevitable. Severe weather events cause billions in property damage annually, and a significant portion of that damage is preventable with straightforward pre-season maintenance. Here is a practical preparation framework you can work through in a weekend.

## Step 1: Assess and Manage Trees Near Your Home

Falling trees and limbs are the leading cause of severe storm damage to homes. Before storm season:

**Trees within striking distance of your home (1.5x tree height):**
- Have an arborist assess trees for structural integrity
- Look for dead limbs, co-dominant stems (V-shaped unions that split in storms), and trees leaning toward your home
- Remove dead trees and dead limbs proactively — do not wait for a storm to decide

**Specific indicators of high-risk trees:**
- Large dead branches in the canopy ("widow-makers")
- Trunk cavities or hollow sections
- Significant lean, especially combined with recent soil saturation
- Root damage from construction, utility work, or compaction
- Fungal growth at the base (often indicates internal decay)

**Pruning vs. removal:** Professional arborists can distinguish between trees that benefit from crown reduction and deadwood removal versus those that are fundamentally structurally compromised. A $500 hazardous tree removal is cheap compared to a $25,000 roof and structural repair after a tree falls on your home.

## Step 2: Clear and Inspect Gutters and Drainage

Clogged gutters turn moderate rainfall events into roof damage events. Water that cannot exit through gutters and downspouts backs up under shingles and fascia boards.

**Pre-storm gutter checklist:**
- [ ] Clean all gutters of debris (leaves, seeds, nests)
- [ ] Flush gutters with a hose to verify downspout flow
- [ ] Check that all hangers are secure — gutters should not sag or pull away from fascia
- [ ] Confirm downspout extensions direct water at least 6 feet from the foundation
- [ ] Clear any debris from window wells and area drains around the property

**Storm-specific drainage concerns:**
- Low spots in the yard that pond water after heavy rain should be addressed with grading or French drains before storm season
- Sump pump should be tested: pour water into the pit and verify it activates and pumps out

## Step 3: Secure Loose Exterior Materials

High-wind events turn any unsecured exterior item into a projectile. Walk your property and inventory:

**Permanently attached items to check:**
- Shutters, screens, and storm windows — confirm all fasteners are tight
- Roof-mounted equipment (satellite dishes, antennas) — verify mounting integrity
- Fence sections — look for loose posts or panels
- Deck furniture — have a secure storage location ready for when storms approach

**Temporary items to plan to move or secure:**
- Patio furniture, umbrellas, and decorative planters
- Children's play equipment if not anchored
- Garbage and recycling bins
- Garden tools, hoses, and outdoor equipment

**A note on timing:** You will not always have lead time before severe weather. Pre-staging a plan — knowing where furniture will go, which garage doors or sheds to open and which items to move first — lets you execute in 20-30 minutes when storm warnings are issued.

## Step 4: Inspect and Address Roof and Exterior Vulnerabilities

Before storm season, have your roof and exterior inspected for conditions that make storm damage more likely or severe.

**DIY ground-level check:**
- Walk the roof perimeter looking for visibly displaced, missing, or curling shingles
- Look at all roof penetrations: chimney, plumbing vents, exhaust vents — check that flashing appears tight
- Look at fascia boards and soffits for rot, gaps, or loose sections
- Check caulking at all wall penetrations: outlets, hose bibs, dryer vents, light fixtures

**Professional inspection items:**
- Flashing at chimney, skylights, and valleys (these are the primary leak points during heavy rain)
- Shingle seal strips — older shingles may have failed adhesive strips, making them vulnerable to wind uplift
- Roof deck condition (requires attic inspection)

If your roof is over 15 years old and has never been inspected, pre-storm season is the right time. A written inspection report documents your roof's condition, which also strengthens any future insurance claim.

## Step 5: Build or Update Your Emergency Kit

Beyond physical preparation, have supplies ready for storm aftermath:

**Essential supplies:**
- Flashlights and backup batteries (avoid candles if power is out — fire risk)
- Battery-powered or hand-crank weather radio
- 3-day supply of water (1 gallon per person per day)
- Non-perishable food and manual can opener
- First aid kit
- Backup power bank for phones
- Cash in small bills (ATMs and card readers fail without power)

**Home emergency supplies:**
- Heavy-duty tarps and rope (for covering damaged roof sections until repairs)
- Waterproof bags for documents
- Photograph and store digital copies of your insurance policy, home inventory, and key documents

**Insurance readiness:**
- Confirm you have your insurance company's claims phone number accessible offline
- Know your policy number and deductible amounts for wind/hail claims
- Complete a current home inventory with photos or video — store in cloud backup

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: When is the best time to do storm preparation?**

Start 4-6 weeks before your region's peak storm season. This gives you time to schedule professional work (arborists, roofers, and contractors get very busy as storm season approaches) and complete DIY tasks without rushing. In most regions, this means late winter to early spring for tornado and thunderstorm season, and early fall for hurricane season in coastal areas.

**Q: Should I file an insurance claim for every storm?**

No. Filing multiple small claims in a short period can affect your renewal rates or insurability. Use insurance for significant structural damage. For smaller cosmetic damage (dented gutters, minor shingle damage), get a contractor estimate first and compare it to your deductible before deciding to file. If the repair cost is close to or below your deductible, it is typically not worth filing.

**Q: What is the first thing to do right after a storm passes?**

Wait for the all-clear from weather services before going outside. Once it is safe, conduct a visual assessment from the ground: look at the roof, check the foundation perimeter, and walk the property to identify any immediate hazards (downed power lines, gas smell, structural damage). Do not enter a structure that appears structurally compromised. Document all visible damage with photos before touching anything — this documentation is important for insurance claims.
